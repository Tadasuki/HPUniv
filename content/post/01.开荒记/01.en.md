Honestly, it’s a bit embarrassing. Every time a major milestone approaches—or more accurately, every time a high-stakes exam looms on the horizon—I get this sudden, restless urge to go "off-script" and do something completely unproductive.

Right now, suffocated by the stifling air of grad school entrance prep, I’ve found myself sitting in front of my laptop, enthusiastically typing out the opening post for this new blog. The scene feels like déjà vu. Thinking back to 2022, during the days I was taking a hiatus from high school and staying at home, I was convinced I was some kind of "chosen" prodigy who could master anything. Back then, in an era before AI was everywhere and you still had to bang your head against a wall to figure things out, I managed to teach myself how to build a website through sheer brute force and a few online tutorials.

I really put in the work back then. It was a true labor of love. To get that blog running smoothly, I spent my days scouring the sites of every tech guru I could find, like a kid scavenging for scraps, digging through mountains of data just to find a single usable line of code. Eventually, I learned how to buy domains and hosting, cobbling together a messy, flashy jumble of features. Looking back, I’m actually a bit impressed by my younger self—starting from zero and having the guts to dive straight into the wilderness. The content back then was purer, mostly extensions of my high school reading notes and excerpts from the greats. I basically built myself a private "literary sanctuary."

The rest of the story is pretty cliché: I went back to school, prepped for the Gaokao, and that blog I’d spent so much time on was left to gather dust. Later, for a variety of reasons too complicated to summarize here (I’ll save those for another time), the old site became a relic—an abandoned house overgrown with weeds.

Fast forward to the present, and the pressure of the grad school entrance exam has arrived right on schedule. Along with it comes that familiar itch to find something—anything—to "escape" into. I thought about reviving the old blog to reconnect with my past self, only to realize that human memory is a fickle thing. Looking at that old Hexo framework was like trying to decipher a dead language. I checked my bookmarks for the gurus I used to follow, but their sites were even more deserted than mine. At that point, I realized I couldn’t even remember where I’d left the keys to the front door.

Whatever. An old house is too hard to fix; better to just start from scratch.

This time, I went with Hugo, which is all the rage these days. I’ve lost that teenage naivety, replaced by a bit more experience. While I wouldn’t say I’m an expert yet, the process was definitely smoother than the first time around when I was practically tearing my hair out. I guess this is the one "dividend" I’ve actually earned from my years in college.

I don’t expect this new garden to attract many visitors. These days, everyone is too busy scrolling through short-form videos and social media. Who has the time to read the ramblings of a neurotic student who’s trying to mimic the prose style of the 1920s? But the lack of an audience is actually quite liberating. This place is my sanctuary—a spot to jot down notes, write essays, and serve as a sort of "external hard drive" for a brain that isn't as sharp as it used to be.

The great scholars Ji Xianlin and Hu Shih weren't afraid to be brutally honest in their diaries about their "unproductive" habits, and I want to emulate that kind of transparency. I’m nowhere near their level—I can’t even see their shadows—but no matter how ordinary I am, I need a little "aimless tinkering" outside of my exams to feel like I’m still vividly alive.

So, let’s leave it at that. Post number one: we’re officially in business.

> Hugo really is better than Hexo.
